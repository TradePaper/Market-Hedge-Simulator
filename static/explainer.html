Figure 1 — Sportsbook Hedging Feasibility Map

Hedging viability depends jointly on sportsbook liability and available event-market liquidity — shallow markets support little or no hedging for large liabilities.

This scales the liquidity thresholds that define the hedging zones. At 1.0×, the boundaries reflect baseline market conditions from the paper. Moving left (below 1.0×) simulates a thinner market where meaningful hedging requires more liquidity relative to liability — the zones compress downward. Moving right (above 1.0×) simulates a deeper market where even large liabilities fall within the partial or meaningful hedging region.

Figure 2 — Liquidity-Constrained Event Risk Transfer Curve

Higher market liquidity flattens the decay of hedgeable fraction — in deep markets, larger liabilities remain meaningfully hedgeable.

k is the liquidity depth parameter for each market regime. It controls how quickly the hedgeable fraction decays as sportsbook liability grows. A low k means hedging capacity drops off steeply — even modest liabilities become largely unhedgeable. A high k means the curve flattens, so larger liabilities remain meaningfully hedgeable. Increase k to simulate a deeper, more liquid market; decrease it to simulate a thinner one.

Figure 3 — Sportsbook Risk Profile Under Hedging

Deep-liquidity hedging compresses worst-case downside materially while preserving a broadly similar expected-value profile.

The hedge ratio represents the fraction of available hedging capacity that the sportsbook actually uses. At 1.0, the book hedges as much as market depth allows. At 0.0, no hedge is placed and all three lines converge to the unhedged outcome. Intermediate values simulate partial hedging — for example, a book that hedges only its largest marquee-event exposures.

Figure 5 — Hedging Efficiency Frontier

Better market depth improves the tradeoff between expected value retained and tail-risk reduced — the deep-liquidity frontier lies outward from the shallow-liquidity frontier.

This scales the slope of both efficiency frontiers simultaneously. At 1.0×, the frontiers reflect the paper's baseline assumptions. Moving right increases the tail-risk reduction achievable per dollar of expected value sacrificed — simulating a market environment where hedging is cheaper and more efficient. Moving left compresses both frontiers toward the origin, simulating a market where hedging is costly relative to the protection it provides.
